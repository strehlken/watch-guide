The Counter-Proposal

You Didn't Like Adolescence.
Fair Enough.

Here's what you should actually be watching — seven shows that do what Adolescence was trying to do, but better, bolder, and without the lecture.

What happened

Someone told you to watch Adolescence. You watched it (or heard enough about it) and something didn't sit right — maybe the moralizing, maybe the framing, maybe the feeling that you were being taught a lesson you didn't ask for. That reaction is valid, and it turns out there's a whole world of television that does the same things Adolescence reaches for — youth in crisis, online radicalization, systemic failure, the gap between what parents see and what's actually happening — with more complexity, more honesty, and a much wider lens. Here are seven of them.

Start Here

The Two That Reframe the Conversation

When They See Us

Netflix
2019 · Limited Series · 4 episodes Ava DuVernay RT 96% MC 80

The Central Park Five — five Black and Latino teenagers accused of a crime they didn't commit, tried in the press before they ever saw a courtroom. DuVernay's four-part series extends the kind of intimate, devastating empathy to these boys that Adolescence reserves for its white protagonist. It's the most direct counterpoint to everything Adolescence is doing, and it aired years earlier.

If Adolescence made you feel like you were watching someone else's panic, this will make you feel like you're watching something that was always yours.

I May Destroy You

HBO / Max
2020 · Limited Series · 12 episodes Michaela Coel RT 98% MC 88

Michaela Coel's series about consent, trauma, and identity in the age of the internet. It deals with online harm and sexual violence with a complexity that makes most other shows on these subjects look like after-school specials. Coel wrote, created, and stars — and the result is one of the most acclaimed shows of the last decade, full stop.

Everything Adolescence wants to say about how the internet warps young people, this show says better — and from a perspective that rarely gets this kind of platform.

The Shows That Got There First

The Wire — Season 4

HBO / Max
2006 · Season 4 of 5 · 13 episodes David Simon RT 98%

Four Black middle-school boys in Baltimore navigating the school-to-prison pipeline. David Simon depicted systemic failure destroying children two decades before Adolescence — with more nuance, more structural understanding, and zero interest in making you feel like a bad parent. Nobody called it "required viewing for every family." They called it great television. It was both.

You can watch Season 4 as a standalone if you haven't done the full series — though once you start, you won't want to stop.

Top Boy

Netflix
2011–2023 · 3 seasons RT 93%

Set on London housing estates — the same country, the same systemic failures, the same young people pulled into violence. Top Boy showed this world for over a decade with Black kids at the center. The response was "great crime drama," never "every parent in Britain must watch this." That framing gap is worth noticing.

Atlanta

Hulu / FX
2016–2022 · 4 seasons Donald Glover RT 97% MC 87

Donald Glover's series about race, masculinity, fatherhood, class, and the surreal texture of Black life in America — without ever reducing any of it to a thesis statement. Where Adolescence says "here's the problem, feel bad," Atlanta says "here's the texture, figure it out yourself." That tonal difference is everything.

If you've missed this, it might be the single best show on this list. Four seasons, all brilliant, each different from the last.

Genre

Same Themes, Wilder Swings

Swarm

Prime Video
2023 · Limited Series · 7 episodes Donald Glover & Janine Nabers RT 79% MC 68

A horror-satire about a Black woman radicalized by internet fandom into violence. It's essentially the mirror image of Adolescence — online obsession warping a young person into something dangerous — but treated as genre horror rather than a parenting PSA. Nobody suggested Oprah co-sign this one.

Polarizing on purpose. The critics who didn't get it are part of the point.

Euphoria

HBO / Max
2019–present · S3 airing now Sam Levinson RT 82% MC 75

Actually does the "teens destroyed by the internet and their own psyches" story — with a diverse cast, visual ambition, and a willingness to be messy and contradictory. It's complicated, it's imperfect, and nobody's serving it to you as homework. It's allowed to just be a show.
