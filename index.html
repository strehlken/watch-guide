Priority

Drop Everything

Beef

Netflix
2023 · Limited Series · 10 episodes MC 86

Steven Yeun and Ali Wong in a dark comedy about a road rage incident that spirals into mutual destruction. It's about race, class, ambition, marriage, and the gap between the life you're performing and the life you're actually living. Funny, vicious, and one of the few prestige shows that centers Asian American leads without making that the "topic."

This should probably have been #1 all along. You two clearly process culture together — you'll be talking about it between episodes.

The Pitt

HBO / Max
2025–present · S2 airing now RT 97–99% MC 85

Noah Wyle in a Pittsburgh ER drama from John Wells. Intense, real, brilliantly paced. Season 2 is sitting at 99% on Rotten Tomatoes.

Great couples watch — dramatic without being gratuitously dark. Best reason to fire up Max right now.

Shōgun

Hulu / FX
2024 · Limited Series RT 99% MC 85

Feudal Japan. Political thriller. Stunning production values. Won a record number of Emmys. It's a slow-burn culture-clash story with a massive payoff.

Sade, if you liked the cerebral tension of something like Arrival — this has that same "dropped into an unfamiliar world" feeling, just set 400 years ago.

Baby Reindeer

Netflix
2024 · Limited Series RT 98% MC 88

A comedian being stalked. Sounds simple — but it's one of the most unsettling, darkly funny, and honest shows in years. Based on a true story.

Where comedy meets horror. This one's going to stick with you both for a while.

Perfect for You Two

Hacks

HBO / Max
Ongoing · S4 airing now RT 99% MC 82

Jean Smart as a legendary comedian mentoring a young comedy writer. Hilarious, sharp, and deeply satisfying — smart comedy with real emotional depth.

For two people who go to comedy clubs together, this is a lock. Best comedy on TV right now.

The White Lotus

HBO / Max
S3 aired early 2025 · Each season standalone RT 90%

Dark comedy thriller set at luxury resorts with razor-sharp writing and an ensemble cast. Darkly funny, morally ambiguous — think Coen Brothers on vacation.

Raising Arizona and O Brother fans, this has that same pitch-black humor with beautiful people doing terrible things.

Poker Face

Peacock
2023 · Procedural RT 97% MC 83

Natasha Lyonne solves mysteries cross-country. Created by Rian Johnson (Knives Out). A modern Columbo — fun, clever, and stylish. Each episode is self-contained, so it's easy to dip in and out.

Your Friends & Neighbors

Apple TV+
2025–present RT 83%

Jon Hamm in a dark comedy crime drama. One of the buzziest shows airing right now — and it's already on your Apple TV+.

The Bear

Hulu / FX
2022–present · 3 seasons RT 96% MC 84

A fine-dining chef inherits his dead brother's Chicago beef sandwich shop. On paper it's about cooking; actually it's about grief, family obligation, and whether excellence is compatible with being a decent person. Intense in a way that has nothing to do with violence or technology — just human pressure. The ensemble is diverse without being self-congratulatory about it.

Industry

HBO / Max
2020–present · S4 airing now RT 96%

Young people destroying themselves in London finance. Multiracial, sexually frank, morally chaotic — it treats its Black female lead (Myha'la Herrold) as a full, complicated, sometimes terrible person rather than a symbol. If you want something that feels adult and propulsive and doesn't care about teaching you a lesson, this is it.

Crossed Off

Considered & Passed

Adolescence

Netflix
2025 · Limited Series · 4 episodes RT 96% MC 91

Technically impressive — four episodes each filmed in a single continuous take, about a 13-year-old accused of murder. The filmmaking craft is genuinely stunning. But the discourse around this show has been heavily politicized: the anti-smartphone parenting movement (the Jonathan Haidt / Anxious Generation crowd) adopted it as Exhibit A, while critics have pointed out that the show's actual subject — incel radicalization and toxic masculinity — is being flattened into a generic "phones bad" narrative that sidesteps questions of gender and race.

We're in the second camp. The show itself may be more nuanced than the conversation around it, but we're not interested in watching it as an anti-phone PSA, and it's hard to unsee the framing once you've seen it. Life's too short — watch Beef instead.
